# inline-skill-definition.py

## Input

What risks should I review for a concentrated equity position?

## Output

### Response

<pre>For a concentrated equity position, review risks across several distinct categories:

1. **Concentration risk**
   - Position size as a percentage of your total portfolio, liquid net worth, and investable assets.
   - Exposure overlap: employer stock, sector funds, options, deferred compensation, or correlated holdings can make concentration larger than it appears.
   - Whether a large drawdown would materially change your financial plans, spending, taxes, or ability to meet obligations.

2. **Market risk**
   - Broad equity-market sensitivity: even a strong company can decline sharply in a market selloff.
   - Sector, factor, country, currency, and interest-rate exposure.
   - Historical downside scenarios—particularly the stock's performance during recessions, sector downturns, and periods of higher rates. History is informative, not predictive.

3. **Company-specific (idiosyncratic) risk**
   - Earnings concentration by product, customer, supplier, geography, or key executive.
   - Competitive disruption, technology shifts, pricing pressure, execution failures, and reputational events.
   - Balance-sheet risks: leverage, refinancing needs, covenant pressure, pension obligations, or weak free-cash-flow conversion.
   - Regulatory, legal, antitrust, data-privacy, product-liability, and governance issues.
   - Valuation risk: high expectations can lead to large declines even if the business continues growing.

4. **Liquidity risk**
   - Average trading volume and bid—ask spreads relative to your position size.
   - Whether selling a meaningful portion could move the share price, especially in stressed markets.
   - Restrictions such as lockups, insider-trading windows, Rule 144 limitations, pledged shares, or contractual transfer limits.
   - If the position is in options or a thinly traded security, assess exit costs and the possibility that liquidity disappears under stress.

5. **Model and estimation risk**
   - DCFs, price targets, volatility estimates, correlation assumptions, and —worst-case— scenarios depend heavily on inputs.
   - Avoid treating backtests or historical volatility as reliable forecasts—regimes change and correlations often rise in selloffs.
   - Stress test assumptions for revenue, margins, discount rates, dilution, credit conditions, and terminal values.

6. **Tax and timing risk**
   - Embedded capital gains, holding-period status, and potential tax consequences of any future reduction.
   - For employer equity: vesting, exercise deadlines, AMT/ordinary-income treatment, blackout periods, and the risk of being forced to act at an unfavorable time.
   - Tax rules are jurisdiction-specific; a tax professional can help quantify them.

7. **Derivatives, leverage, and collateral risk**
   - Options, margin loans, prepaid forwards, collars, or stock-secured borrowing can introduce expiration, counterparty, margin-call, basis, and forced-sale risks.
   - A hedge can limit one risk while adding others—cost, upside caps, rollover risk, and imperfect protection.

8. **Behavioral and governance risk**
   - Familiarity bias, anchoring to a past peak price, loyalty to an employer, and reluctance to realize gains or losses.
   - Pre-commitment to risk limits and review triggers can reduce emotionally driven decisions.
   - For a business owner or insider, assess whether personal income and career risk are already tied to the same company or industry.

Useful review metrics include: percentage of portfolio and net worth in the position, correlation with the rest of the portfolio and income, daily trading volume versus shares held, cost basis and unrealized gain, debt/maturity profile, valuation relative to plausible operating outcomes, and stress-test loss amounts rather than only percentages.

This is research, not a recommendation to buy, hold, hedge, or sell. The key question is whether the position's plausible downside is compatible with your financial objectives, liquidity needs, tax situation, and ability to withstand a prolonged decline.
</pre>
